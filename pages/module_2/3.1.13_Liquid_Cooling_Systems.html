3.1.13 Liquid Cooling Systems

When Is Liquid Cooling Used?

Liquid cooling is used when air cooling is insufficient. It is common in:

  • High-end gaming PCs
  • High-performance workstations
  • Systems operating in high ambient temperatures

Liquid cooling pumps water around the chassis, offering more effective cooling than air convection and often operating more quietly than multiple fans.

Open-Loop vs Closed-Loop

Type Also Known As Components Cooled Complexity
Open-loop Custom loop Multiple components (CPU, GPU, etc.) More complex; fully customisable
Closed-loop AIO (All-In-One) cooler Single component only (CPU or GPU) Simpler; pre-filled and sealed

Open-Loop System Components

Component Function
Water loop / tubing Carries coolant around the system
Pump Pushes coolant through the loop
Reservoir Holds the coolant supply; where coolant is added
Water blocks and brackets Attached to each device; remove heat by convection (similar to a heatsink); connected to the water loop
Radiators and fans Positioned at air vents to dispel excess heat from the coolant

Maintenance

  • Periodically drain, clean, and refill the open-loop system
  • Keep fans and radiators dust-free for optimal performance
  • Drain the system before moving the PC to a different location to prevent leaks

Key Terms

  • Liquid cooling – A cooling method that pumps water or coolant through the chassis to absorb and dissipate heat
  • Open-loop system – A fully customisable liquid cooling setup with separate pump, reservoir, tubing, water blocks, and radiator
  • Closed-loop system (AIO) – A pre-filled, sealed liquid cooler designed for a single component such as the CPU or GPU
  • Water block – A component attached to a device that absorbs heat and transfers it to the coolant
  • Reservoir – The chamber that holds the coolant supply in an open-loop system
  • Radiator – Dissipates heat from the coolant back into the air, assisted by fans
