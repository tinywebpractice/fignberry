3.1.7 Redundant Power Supplies

What is a Redundant Power Supply?

A redundant power supply setup equips a system with two PSUs. One operates normally while the other acts as a failover — if the primary PSU fails, the second immediately takes over, minimizing downtime and preventing data loss.

Where Are They Used?

Environment Redundant PSU Used? Reason
Data Centers Yes — standard feature Uninterrupted service is essential
High-availability servers Yes — standard feature Continuous operation and data integrity required
Desktop computers Uncommon Not required to maintain server-level uptime

How It Works in Servers

  • Each PSU connects to a backplane — a circuit board providing electrical connections between components
  • The backplane enables hot-swappable PSUs, meaning a faulty unit can be replaced without powering down the system or opening the case
  • This ensures critical services remain available during PSU failure or maintenance

Key Terms

  • Redundant PSU – A second PSU that takes over if the primary fails
  • Failover – The automatic switching to a backup PSU when the primary fails
  • Backplane – A circuit board that provides electrical connections between components, including PSUs in a server
  • Hot-swappable – The ability to replace a component (e.g. a PSU) without shutting down or interrupting the system
  • Uptime – The amount of time a system remains operational without interruption
