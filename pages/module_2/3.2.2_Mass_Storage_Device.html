3.2.2 Mass Storage Devices

What is Mass Storage?

Mass storage refers to non-volatile storage devices that retain data even when the system is powered off. These devices use magnetic, optical, or solid-state technology. Internal mass storage devices are called fixed disks.

Drive Bay Sizes

Bay Size Typical Use Notes
5.25 inch Optical drives (DVD), smart card readers Often has a removable front panel
3.5 inch Hard drives (HDD) Standard desktop drive bay
2.5 inch Laptop HDDs and SSDs Can be installed in a 3.5-inch bay using an adapter caddy

Drive Caddies

  • Caddies allow secure mounting of drives in bays and can adapt different drive sizes to fit various bays
  • Example: a 2.5-inch drive can be installed in a 3.5-inch bay using an adapter caddy
  • Some caddies use rails for easy drive removal without opening the case

External Storage Devices

Removable and external storage devices are used for backup, data transfer, and additional storage. They typically connect via USB or Thunderbolt ports.

Factors When Choosing Mass Storage

Factor What to Consider
Reliability Risk of total device failure and partial data corruption; varies by technology type
Performance Read/write speed, sequential vs. random access, data throughput (MB/s or GB/s), and IOPS (input/output operations per second)
Use case Running an OS, hosting a database, streaming audio/video, removable media, or data backup and archiving

Key Terms

  • Mass storage – Non-volatile storage that retains data without power; uses magnetic, optical, or solid-state technology
  • Fixed disk – An internal mass storage device installed inside the computer chassis
  • Drive bay – A slot in the chassis designed to hold a storage drive of a specific size
  • Caddy – A mounting bracket that secures a drive in a bay and can adapt different drive sizes
  • IOPS – Input/Output Operations Per Second; a measure of storage performance
  • Throughput – The rate at which data is read from or written to a drive, measured in MB/s or GB/s
