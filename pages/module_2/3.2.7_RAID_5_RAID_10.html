3.2.7 RAID 5 and RAID 10

RAID 5 – Striping with Distributed Parity

RAID 5 combines striping (like RAID 0) with distributed parity. Error correction information is spread across all disks in the array. Data and parity information are always stored on different disks. If one disk fails, data can be reconstructed from the remaining disks.

Property Detail
Minimum disks 3 (can use more)
Fault tolerance 1 disk failure — data can be reconstructed from parity
Read performance Excellent normally; slows down during disk failure (parity rebuild)
Write performance Slower — parity must be calculated on every write
Usable capacity Total capacity minus the equivalent of one disk (used for parity)
Best used for Balanced performance, capacity, and redundancy

Note: Fault tolerance and usable space are inversely related — more disks means more usable space but a greater chance of failure. If more than one disk fails, the entire volume becomes unavailable.

RAID 5 Parity Overhead Example

Number of Disks Parity Overhead Example (80 GB each)
3 disks 1/3 of each disk 160 GB usable (240 GB total)
4 disks 1/4 of each disk 240 GB usable (320 GB total)

RAID 10 – Stripe of Mirrors

RAID 10 is a nested RAID level that combines RAID 1 (mirroring) and RAID 0 (striping). It creates a striped volume made up of mirrored pairs. One disk in each mirrored pair can fail without bringing down the entire volume.

Property Detail
Minimum disks 4 (must be an even number)
Fault tolerance 1 disk per mirrored pair can fail without data loss
Read performance Excellent — benefits from both striping and mirroring
Write performance Good — no parity calculation needed
Usable capacity 50% of total disk space (50% overhead for mirroring)
Best used for High performance and high redundancy environments

RAID 5 vs RAID 10 Comparison

Feature RAID 5 RAID 10
Method Striping + distributed parity Striping of mirrored pairs
Min. drives 3 4 (even number)
Fault tolerance 1 disk failure 1 disk per mirrored pair
Usable capacity Total minus 1 disk equivalent 50% of total
Write speed Slower (parity calculation) Faster (no parity)
Cost efficiency Better Higher cost (50% overhead)

Key Terms

  • Distributed parity – Error correction data spread evenly across all disks in the array (used in RAID 5)
  • Parity – Mathematical error-checking data used to reconstruct lost data if a disk fails
  • RAID 5 – Striping with distributed parity; min. 3 disks; tolerates 1 disk failure
  • RAID 10 – Nested RAID combining RAID 0 striping and RAID 1 mirroring; min. 4 disks; excellent fault tolerance and performance
  • Nested RAID – A RAID configuration that combines two RAID levels (e.g. RAID 10 = RAID 1 + RAID 0)
  • Sub-volume – A mirrored pair within a RAID 10 array that acts as one unit in the stripe
