3.2.8 RAID 6 – Striping with Double Parity

What is RAID 6?

RAID 6 uses striping with dual distributed parity — two sets of parity information are spread across all disks. This allows the array to survive the simultaneous failure of two disks, providing greater fault tolerance than RAID 5.

It is ideal for environments with higher disk failure risks, such as large arrays or critical systems.

RAID 6 Properties

Property Detail
Minimum disks 4 (2 for data, 2 for parity)
Fault tolerance 2 simultaneous disk failures — array remains operational
Read performance Good — similar to RAID 5
Write performance Slower than RAID 5 — two sets of parity must be calculated and written
Usable capacity Total capacity minus the equivalent of two disks (used for parity)
Cost More expensive than RAID 5 — requires more disks and a more powerful controller
Best used for Large arrays and critical systems where two-disk failure protection is needed

Usable Capacity Example

Array Configuration Total Capacity Parity Overhead Usable Capacity
4 x 1 TB disks 4 TB 2 TB (2 disks for parity) 2 TB usable

RAID 5 vs RAID 6

Feature RAID 5 RAID 6
Parity sets 1 2
Min. drives 3 4
Disk failures tolerated 1 2
Write speed Slower than RAID 0 Slower than RAID 5
Cost Lower Higher
Best for Moderate arrays Large or critical arrays

Maintenance Considerations

  • Array continues operating if 1 or 2 disks fail, but performance degrades
  • Rebuild times are longer than RAID 5 — time-consuming based on disk size and system load
  • Monitor array health regularly and plan for prompt disk replacements

Key Terms

  • RAID 6 – Striping with dual distributed parity; tolerates 2 simultaneous disk failures; minimum 4 disks
  • Dual distributed parity – Two independent sets of parity data spread across all disks in the array
  • Write penalty – The performance cost of calculating and writing parity data on every write operation
  • Rebuild – The process of reconstructing lost data onto a replacement disk using remaining parity information
